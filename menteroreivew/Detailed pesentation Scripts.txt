Detailed Presentation Scripts

Slide 1 – Title
“Good afternoon. Today I will review the Hybrid Quantum-Agentic AML system 
that was constructed on the basis of the Technical Brief and the Mathematical 
Derivation. I will examine it through the lens of the Mentorship Proposal and 
the Expert Evaluation Report, focusing on architectural bottlenecks and 
concrete next steps. This is a 20-minute technical briefing.”

Slide 2 – Agenda
“This technical briefing is organized into seven sections. We begin with 
motivation and scope, move to the overall architecture and mathematical core, 
then cover the QUBO–Ising–QAOA pipeline, the multi-agent compliance layer, 
the three key bottlenecks identified in the mentorship feedback, the expert 
evaluation highlights, and finally the recommended refinements and next 
steps.”

Slide 3 – Motivation
“There are three fundamental reasons why a graph-based quantum approach 
is needed. First, a suspicious wallet often appears completely normal when 
examined in isolation; the pattern only becomes visible across multi-hop fund 
flows. Second, multi-hop transaction networks create a combinatorial explosion 
that classical exhaustive search cannot scale. Third, compliance teams are 
overwhelmed by false positives. That is why the objective function is 
deliberately designed to prioritize precision with β equal to 0.5.”

Slide 4 – Threat Model & NISQ Scope
“The threat model covers structuring and smurfing, multi-hop layering, 
cross-border and cross-chain obfuscation, exposure to mixers, bridges and 
sanctioned entities, and rapid fund movement. Under current NISQ 
constraints, if only 20 qubits are available, the full transaction graph is 
reduced by classical modularization into a high-risk Top-20 subgraph. 
QAOA(Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm) is then applied only to 


that focused combinatorial core. This keeps the quantum workload realistic 
while preserving the essential AML logic.”

Slide 5 – Architecture Overview
“The end-to-end pipeline begins with wallet-address validation, constructs a 
directed on-chain transaction graph, extracts eleven risk features, selects the 
Top-20 high-risk subgraph, formulates the QUBO matrix, solves it with QAOA, 
enriches the results with multi-agent investigation, and finally issues a verdict 
together with a SHA-256-hashed audit report. Quantum resources are 
reserved exclusively for the binary flagging decision on the Top-20 wallets.”

Slide 6 – Graph Construction & Features
“The transaction data is represented as a directed graph G equals (V, E), 
where each edge carries amount, timestamp and chain information. From the 
local neighborhood of each wallet we extract a set of raw AML features: 
amount anomaly relative to peer median and MAD, transaction frequency, 
structuring ratio, rapid-flow velocity, sequential in-out ratio, network centrality 
composite, mixer or bridge flags, and maximum external counterparty risk.”

Slide 7 – Normalization & Node Risk Score
“All continuous features are normalized with a robust percentile method 
anchored at the median and the 95th percentile, then clipped to the unit 
interval. A dynamic masking vector prevents missing data from introducing 
bias. The masked weighted average produces a single node risk score which is 
again clipped to the range zero to one. This score becomes the primary 
classical signal for both subgraph selection and the linear term of the QUBO.”

Slide 8 – Top-20 Subgraph Modularization
“A classical priority score combines the normalized node risk, centrality, path 
risk and an indicator that forces the seed wallet to remain inside the 
subgraph. The Top-20 wallets selected by this score form the quantum-ready 
subgraph. One wallet corresponds to one qubit and one binary decision 
variable. This modularization is the practical bridge between a potentially huge 


transaction network and near-term quantum hardware.”

Slide 9 – QUBO Formulation
“The global objective is expressed as the quadratic form C of x equals 
x-transpose Q x. The linear coefficient rewards the flagging of high-risk 
wallets while applying an explicit penalty for known licit entities; the 
precision-oriented F-beta term with beta equal to 0.5 is built into this 
coefficient. The pairwise term simultaneously encourages genuine risk coupling 
and suppresses over-flagging on weakly evidenced edges through an explicit 
false-positive penalty.”

Slide 10 – Ising Mapping
“Binary variables are converted to spin variables by the standard identity xi 
equals (1 minus zi) over 2. Expanding the quadratic form yields a constant 
term, linear fields hi, and coupling strengths Jij. The resulting cost Hamiltonian 
is a sum of single-qubit Z terms and two-qubit ZZ interactions. The constant 
is omitted from the quantum circuit because it does not affect the location of 
the minimum-energy bit-string.”

Slide 11 – QAOA Execution
“QAOA alternates applications of the cost Hamiltonian and the transverse-field 
mixer. Classical optimization finds the parameters gamma-star and beta-star 
that minimize the expected energy. Measurement yields bit-strings; the best 
sampled solution together with the marginal flag probability estimated from all 
shots are combined into a single quantum evidence score zeta-Q. This score 
is passed to the downstream classical agents.”

Slide 12 – Multi-Agent Compliance Layer
“Three specialized agents are deployed. The Flow Tracer reconstructs 
multi-hop fund paths and quantifies velocity and concentration. The OSINT 
Analyst queries external databases such as OpenSanctions and CryptoScamDB 
and returns normalized external risk signals. The Compliance Officer 
aggregates the quantum evidence with these off-chain signals and applies the 


deterministic decision rules. This layer supplies the classical explainability that 
pure quantum output inherently lacks.”

Slide 13 – Final Risk Score & Decision Rules
“The final compliance score is a convex combination of the classical node 
risk, the quantum evidence score, and the external intelligence signals, clipped 
to the unit interval. If the score exceeds the high threshold or a confirmed 
sanctions hit is present, the system issues FREEZE. Scores in the intermediate 
band produce MONITOR; scores below the low threshold produce CLEAR. All 
weights and thresholds are calibrated on historical labelled cases.”

Slide 14 – Bottleneck 1: Stealth / Smurfing
“Fragmented micro-transactions are the financial analogue of a 
low-radar-cross-section target. Just as MIMO spatial multiplexing and coherent 
integration recover a stealthy radar return from the noise floor, we propose 
to treat the wallet-by-time interaction as a tensor and apply multi-dimensional 
decomposition together with spatial correlation algorithms. 
This clustering step can reassemble seemingly disconnected smurfed transfers 
into a single high-probability threat signature before the QUBO stage.”

Slide 15 – Bottleneck 2: Interception Latency
“In the current design the QAOA output is routed through a multi-agent 
CrewAI workflow before a FREEZE decision is issued. Multi-agent LLM 
frameworks are inherently non-deterministic and introduce seconds-to-minutes 
of latency—unacceptable for real-time interception. 
Drawing on the CIWS model, we recommend decoupling the kill chain from 
the analysis chain: the QAOA optimizer itself should trigger an immediate 
automated freeze, while the agents run in parallel solely to generate the 
audit-ready report.”


Slide 16 – Bottleneck 3: Physical-Layer Anti-Spoofing
“If an adversary knows that the system relies on public endpoints such as 
Etherscan or The Graph, it can inject Sybil data or exploit RPC delays. 
We therefore propose a data-verification layer that performs multi-node RPC 
consensus, cross-verifies state hashes, and enforces cryptographic payload 
signing. 
Telemetry that fails the integrity threshold is treated as jammed or spoofed 
and is routed to a fallback redundancy pipeline. Hard timeouts on the 
orchestrator prevent pipeline blocking.”

Slide 17 – Expert Evaluation Highlights
“The external expert evaluation confirmed several strengths. The 
graph-to-QUBO mapping is regarded as a highly effective way to bring 
quantum optimization to financial security. 
Feature engineering with dynamic masking and robust normalization is judged 
mathematically sound. 
The Top-20 modularization is viewed as a practical and principled response to 
NISQ constraints. 
Finally, the multi-agent layer is recognized as essential for restoring classical 
explainability and producing a cryptographically auditable report.”

Slide 18 – Recommended Refinements
“Two concrete refinements are recommended before the final pitch. 
First, the QAOA depth parameter p must be explicitly bounded and the 
noise-versus-accuracy trade-off curve demonstrated on the target hardware 
or a high-fidelity simulator. 
Second, a sensitivity analysis of the classical weights and decision thresholds 
should be performed so that the robustness of the final verdict distribution 
can be quantified and presented.”
